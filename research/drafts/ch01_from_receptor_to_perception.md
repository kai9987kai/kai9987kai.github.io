# Chapter 1 Draft: From Receptor Binding to Altered Perception

## Working Premise

A psychedelic experience can feel metaphysical, but it begins as a molecular event.

A compound binds to receptors on neurons. That binding changes signaling. Signaling changes excitability and timing. Timing changes how networks coordinate. Network coordination changes what the brain treats as signal, noise, self, and world.

This chapter follows that chain from the first binding event to the first subjective shifts.

## 1. The First Contact: Receptor Engagement

For classical psychedelics, the key receptor is typically framed as `5-HT2A` (a serotonin receptor), especially on cortical pyramidal neurons [Nichols2016; Preller2018].

That does not mean:

- `5-HT2A` is the only receptor involved
- every subjective effect maps one-to-one onto a single receptor
- all compounds in the "hallucinogen" category work the same way

It does mean `5-HT2A` is a central entry point for explaining why perception, cognition, and self-processing begin to destabilize in characteristic ways.

### Why binding is not just "on/off"

Receptors are not simple switches. Different ligands can:

- bind with different affinity
- stay bound for different durations (residence time)
- stabilize different receptor conformations
- bias downstream signaling toward different intracellular pathways

This is the start of why LSD, psilocin, and other compounds can feel qualitatively different even when they share overlapping receptor targets. For LSD specifically, unusually slow dissociation kinetics and an extracellular loop "lid" mechanism are strongly supported in receptor-structure/kinetics work, though not sufficient on their own to explain the full subjective time course [Wacker2017].

## 2. Cortical Amplification: Layer 5 Pyramidal Neurons

Once receptor signaling changes in cortex, the effects do not stay local.

Layer 5 pyramidal neurons are especially important because they are long-range output neurons. They project to other cortical and subcortical regions and can influence how information flows through larger loops.

A useful simplification is:

- receptor perturbation changes gain and excitability in key cortical populations (model-supported) [Muthukumaraswamy2013]
- cortical outputs become less constrained and less synchronized in ordinary patterns
- downstream systems receive "noisier" or less predictably gated input

This does not automatically produce visuals or ego dissolution. But it sets the conditions for them by changing the reliability of top-down control [Muthukumaraswamy2013; CarhartHarrisFriston2019]. The predictive-processing framing here is a useful framework rather than a settled proof of every intermediate step.

## 3. The Thalamus Problem: Filtering Starts to Loosen

The thalamus acts as a relay and filtering hub for information headed to cortex. In ordinary waking consciousness, it helps prioritize what reaches awareness and what stays in the background.

When cortical-thalamic loops are perturbed, the system may become less selective:

- irrelevant sensory detail gains access
- internal signals compete more effectively with external signals
- attentional selection becomes unstable

This is a better starting point than "the brain sees things that are not there." In many cases, altered states begin as changes in filtering, salience, and interpretation before they become explicit hallucinations.

## 4. Rhythm Collapse and Reweighting

Perception is not only about where signals go. It is also about when they arrive.

The brain depends on rhythmic coordination across regions. Under psychedelics, one recurring finding is disruption of ordinary oscillatory organization (especially reduced alpha power in many paradigms), alongside altered coupling and changing relationships between top-down and bottom-up signaling [Kometer2013; Muthukumaraswamy2013].

Subjectively, this can feel like:

- sensory intensity
- fragmentation or fluidity of thought
- unusual pattern detection
- time distortion
- unstable boundaries between "inside" and "outside"

At this stage, the experience often becomes unmistakable: the brain is no longer enforcing the same predictive constraints at the same strength (framework interpretation) [CarhartHarrisFriston2019].

## 5. Why Meaning Changes So Fast

One of the most underestimated effects is not visual distortion, but salience distortion.

A face, a memory, a song, or a passing thought can feel overwhelmingly important. This suggests that psychedelics alter the brain's assignment of precision and relevance, not just its sensory rendering (framework interpretation) [CarhartHarrisFriston2019].

That has two consequences:

1. It can drive insight-like experiences and emotional breakthrough.
2. It can also drive fear, paranoia, or over-interpretation if the context is unstable.

This duality is central to the rest of the project. The same loosening of constraints that enables new learning can also destabilize the system.

## 6. A Practical Scientific Caution

The "chain of events" described above is a model, not a single proven pathway.

Different compounds, doses, brains, and settings produce different trajectories. The point of this framework is not to eliminate mystery. It is to connect levels of explanation so we can ask better questions.

The rest of the project will test and refine this chain:

- Which links are strongly supported?
- Which are indirect inferences?
- Which differ across compounds or clinical uses?
