# Chapter 2 Draft: How the Thalamus Loses the Room

## Why Start With the Thalamus?

If Chapter 1 explains how cortical signaling begins to destabilize, Chapter 2 explains how that destabilization becomes experience.

The thalamus is often described as a relay station, but that undersells its role. A better framing is "relay-plus-control": thalamic circuitry participates in routing, amplification, suppression, and coordination of what cortex can treat as relevant at a given moment. That framing comes primarily from systems-neuroscience reviews and conceptual/anatomical work, and only secondarily from psychedelic imaging papers [Sherman2007; GuillerySherman2002; Preller2018].

- routing sensory information to cortex
- shaping which signals are amplified or suppressed
- coordinating with cortex to maintain attention and state

When psychedelic effects become unmistakable, part of what has changed is not only what signals exist, but which ones are allowed through, how they are weighted, and which loops dominate the competition for access to awareness.

## 1. The Baseline: Normal Gating Is Active Work

Ordinary consciousness feels effortless because the filtering is mostly invisible.

At any moment, your nervous system is flooded with data:

- interoceptive signals (heartbeat, gut activity, breathing)
- exteroceptive signals (sound, light, touch)
- memory fragments and internal imagery
- predictive models from higher cortical areas

Most of this does not enter focal awareness. Thalamocortical circuits, attention networks, and inhibitory control systems help prevent overload.

This matters because altered states are often described as "more information." In practice, they may be better described as altered selection, altered competition, and altered salience.

### Why "gatekeeper" is built into thalamic design

The gating language is not just metaphor. It follows from the architecture.

Systems-neuroscience accounts emphasize that thalamic relay function is heavily shaped by modulatory inputs (state, arousal, attention, corticothalamic context), not only by "driver" inputs carrying primary content [Sherman2007; GuillerySherman2002]. These sources are theoretical/systems frameworks grounded in anatomy and physiology, not psychedelic-specific experiments. In other words, the thalamus is continuously receiving instructions about how to pass information, not just what information to pass.

A chapter-friendly distinction here is:

- first-order relays: primarily driven by ascending sensory pathways (for example, the LGN in vision)
- higher-order relays: embedded in cortico-thalamo-cortical communication and therefore well positioned to regulate corticocortical routing (for example, pulvinar/mediodorsal thalamus); this is supported by conceptual reviews plus primate attention-task physiology [Sherman2007; GuillerySherman2002; Saalmann2012; SaalmannKastner2011]

This makes "effortless consciousness" less mysterious. The system feels effortless because gain, routing, and timing conditions are being set continuously by distributed control signals before most content ever reaches focal awareness.

## 2. The CSTC Loop as a Useful Model

A common framework for psychedelic sensory and cognitive effects is disruption within cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) loops [Nichols2016; Preller2018; VollenweiderGeyer2001]. Here the evidence stack is mixed by design: review/theory papers provide the circuit scaffold, while human imaging provides indirect systems-level support. Historically, "thalamic filter" models were used to explain psychosis-like overload and fragmentation; in psychedelic neuroscience, the same scaffold remains useful as long as it is treated as a model, not a single settled pathway.

The exact details vary by paper and compound, but the broad idea is consistent:

1. Cortical activity patterns are perturbed (especially in association cortex).
2. Downstream loop dynamics involving striatum and thalamus change.
3. Thalamic gating and cortical feedback become less selective or differently weighted.
4. Awareness becomes less constrained by ordinary filtering rules.

This framework is useful because it explains why the effects are not purely visual. If gating and loop dynamics are altered, then sensory, cognitive, emotional, and self-referential processing can all shift together [Nichols2016; Preller2018].

### Directed connectivity matters, not just "more noise"

One reason the CSTC framing remains useful is that psychedelic effects can be modeled as changes in directed influence, not only undifferentiated signal amplification. In fMRI-based effective-connectivity modeling (DCM), LSD has been associated with direction-specific CSTC changes (including thalamus-related directed effects linked to `5-HT2A` signaling), which supports a "reweighted pathway influence" view over a simple "filter failed everywhere" story [Preller2019PNAS]. Because this is a model-based inference layer on top of fMRI, it should be described as stronger than raw metaphor but weaker than direct causal circuit recording.

## 3. Human Imaging: Thalamocortical Reconfiguration Is Real, but Not a Single Scalar

The strongest human anchor for this chapter remains placebo-controlled, ketanserin-blockade LSD imaging (resting-state fMRI connectivity analyses) showing that `5-HT2A` blockade attenuates both subjective effects and thalamic/global connectivity changes [Preller2018]. That gives the chapter an important mechanistic bridge: thalamocortical reconfiguration is not just nonspecific arousal.

At the same time, newer work sharpens an important nuance: "thalamic connectivity increased" is often too coarse.

- Whole-thalamus and nucleus-resolved resting-state fMRI analyses under LSD suggest selective increases in connectivity for particular nuclei and pathways (especially sensory-linked channels), not a uniform increase across all thalamic outputs [Mueller2017; DelliPizzi2023].
- In nucleus-resolved resting-state analyses, some studies also report reduced striatal-thalamic coupling alongside increased thalamocortical coupling, which fits a CSTC reweighting narrative better than a generic gain increase [DelliPizzi2023].
- In resting-state correlation analyses, thalamocortical connectivity measures have been reported to covary with subjective auditory/visual alteration intensity in LSD datasets, making the chapter's "weighting" language more defensible than a purely anatomical description [Mueller2017].

Psilocybin adds another useful constraint: results can look different depending on whether thalamus is treated as one region of interest or as a set of functionally distinct components. In resting-state fMRI component-decomposition analyses, localized effects (including mediodorsal/pulvinar-related components) show mixed directionality and subjective correlations, which is exactly why "filter failure everywhere" is weaker than "selective routing/gain reconfiguration" [Gaddis2022].

More recent high-resolution and precision-mapping work supports the broad thesis (psychedelics disrupt subcortical-cortical organization) while reinforcing the same warning: the effect is not captured by one scalar such as "more thalamic connectivity." Across recent high-density longitudinal fMRI, 7T crossover fMRI, and comparative pharmacology fMRI datasets, patterns include within-network reductions, between-network increases, desynchronization across scales, and subcortical signals (including thalamic contributions) that vary with subjective experience [Siegel2024Nature; Mallaroni2026; Avram2025MolPsych].

## 4. From Filtering Failure to Hallucinatory Pressure

"Hallucination" is often treated as an on/off event, but many experiences build in stages:

- increased vividness
- enhanced detail or texture
- unstable figure-ground separation
- pattern completion (faces, geometry, symbolic forms)
- intrusion of internally generated imagery into perception

The thalamic-gating model helps explain this progression (model-level explanation; see evidence boundaries in `research/source_map.md`).

If the system becomes less efficient at separating signal classes, then:

- weak sensory noise can be amplified
- internal imagery can compete with external input
- attention can lock onto self-generated patterns
- prediction and perception can become harder to distinguish

This does not require the brain to "invent everything from nothing." It requires a change in the rules for what counts as relevant input.

### Behavioral gating assays are useful, but not interchangeable

There is convergent behavioral evidence that serotonergic psychedelics can perturb inhibitory filtering in humans and animals, but the measures are not interchangeable and the results are parameter-sensitive [Nichols2016; VollenweiderCsomor2007; Riba2002]. Here the methods are behavioral/psychophysiological assays (for example, startle prepulse inhibition and P50 suppression), not direct measurements of thalamic circuitry.

This matters for the chapter because it supports a stronger claim and avoids a weak one:

- stronger claim: psychedelics can alter gating-related behavior in measurable ways
- weaker claim: every gating assay reflects one latent "thalamic filter" variable

Some paradigms show dissociations (for example, one gating metric changes while another does not), which is exactly what we would expect if gating is implemented by distributed circuits and task-dependent dynamics rather than a single switch [Riba2002]. These assays are best treated as convergent operational proxies for gating-related control, not one-to-one readouts of a single thalamic mechanism.

## 5. The Thalamic Reticular Nucleus (TRN): A Likely Lever, Not a Solved Answer

The thalamic reticular nucleus (TRN) is often discussed because it makes the gating metaphor anatomically literal. It is a thin shell of GABAergic neurons surrounding much of the dorsal thalamus, receiving collaterals from thalamocortical and corticothalamic axons and sending inhibitory projections back to thalamic relay nuclei [PrattMorris2015; Pinault2004; GuilleryFeigLozsadi1998]. This claim is anchored in anatomy/physiology reviews rather than psychedelic datasets.

In simple terms, it is positioned as an inhibitory interface that can shape what gets through and when.

Animal and primate work also links TRN and higher-order thalamic nuclei (especially pulvinar) to selective attention and coordination of cortical communication, including attention-dependent modulation of effective transmission and synchrony between cortical areas [Saalmann2012; McAlonan2006; Halassa2014]. Methodologically, this evidence comes from task-based primate electrophysiology and animal circuit physiology, which gives mechanistic leverage but limited direct psychedelic specificity.

That makes TRN a plausible mechanistic lever for psychedelic sensory gating changes. But a careful phrasing is important: direct psychedelic TRN-specific evidence is weaker than the broader thalamic-gating evidence summarized in current drafts, so this should remain a candidate mechanism rather than a settled claim (see gap note in `research/source_map.md`).

- it is a strong candidate within gating models
- it is not a single proven switch that explains all psychedelic phenomena

A good scientific version of the story is to treat TRN involvement as part of a distributed gating system that includes:

- TRN-mediated inhibition
- nucleus-specific thalamic properties (including higher-order relays such as pulvinar/mediodorsal thalamus)
- corticothalamic feedback
- broader neuromodulatory state (serotonin, noradrenaline, dopamine)

There is also plausible mechanistic support for serotonergic influence at this level: serotonergic modulation has been documented in thalamic/TRN circuitry, and `5-HT2A` signaling can alter how thalamocortical inputs impact cortical targets in experimental preparations [Rodriguez2011; Goitia2016; Barre2016; MoncktonMcCormick2002; VarelaSherman2009; Reggiani2023]. This evidence spans rodent anatomy, neurochemistry, slice/synaptic plasticity, and systems physiology rather than human psychedelic neuroimaging. These findings strengthen plausibility, but they do not by themselves prove a TRN-specific psychedelic mechanism in humans.

## 6. Why the Experience Feels "Open" and "Flooded"

Subjective reports often include:

- sensory flooding
- porous boundaries between thought and sensation
- heightened emotional resonance
- simultaneous relevance of too many things

These reports make sense if two processes happen together:

1. Gating becomes less selective.
2. Precision/salience assignment becomes unstable.

That combination is potent. More signals compete for access, and more of them feel important once they get in.

This is where Chapter 2 can connect cleanly to predictive-processing language without overclaiming causality. Contemporary frameworks such as REBUS interpret psychedelic states as relaxing the precision weighting of higher-level priors, increasing the influence of bottom-up and internally generated signals [CarhartHarrisFriston2019]. That citation is a computational/theoretical framework, not a thalamus-specific experiment. In this chapter, the thalamus can be framed as one structural site where parts of that weighting/gain problem become physically instantiated through routing, synchrony, and competition across channels.

This is also why the same pharmacological perturbation can produce opposite subjective interpretations:

- wonder ("everything is connected")
- threat ("everything is coming at me")

The gating change may be similar; the appraisal context is not.

Kometer's psilocybin electrophysiology work is a particularly useful bridge here because it links `5-HT2A`-mediated effects to alpha-band and visual processing changes in humans (EEG/ERP and psychophysiology), helping connect receptor pharmacology to a putative gating rhythm before Chapter 3 fully shifts into oscillations [Kometer2013].

## 7. What This Model Explains Well (and What It Does Not)

### Strengths

The thalamic/CSTC framing helps explain:

- multisensory effects
- attentional instability
- perceptual amplification before full hallucination
- overlap between sensory, emotional, and cognitive changes
- why nucleus-specific and pathway-specific connectivity changes can matter more than whole-thalamus averages
- why directed connectivity changes (not only amplitude changes) are relevant to altered self/perception dynamics
- how different methods converge on the same broad theme (resting-state FC, DCM, gating assays, attention physiology) while still measuring different things

### Limits

It does not fully explain:

- why specific visual motifs recur (geometry, faces, patterns)
- why some compounds are more emotional vs perceptual
- why some effects persist after the acute state
- therapeutic outcomes months later
- why different gating assays (PPI, P50, task-based attention measures) can dissociate across paradigms
- which portions of the narrative are specific to resting-state fMRI correlations versus directed-model inferences or animal physiology

Those require additional layers:

- cortical hierarchy and predictive coding
- network dynamics and oscillations
- memory reconsolidation and plasticity
- psychosocial context and learning

In other words, thalamic gating is part of the bridge, not the whole map.

## 8. Implications for the Broader Thesis

If the thalamus helps enforce controlled access to consciousness, then psychedelic states can be understood as partial relaxation of access control.

That does not make the brain "more truthful" or "more random" in any simple sense. It makes the system less constrained and more sensitive to both signal and self-generated noise.

This is exactly the kind of state that can produce:

- novel associations
- destabilization of rigid beliefs
- sensory distortions
- emotional breakthrough
- dysregulation and panic

The same door opens to all of them.

## Bridge to Chapter 3

Chapter 2 explains how the gate loosens. Chapter 3 should explain what happens to the brain's timing and predictive machinery once the gate is no longer enforcing normal priorities [Kometer2013; Muthukumaraswamy2013; Alamia2020; CarhartHarrisFriston2019]. In that bridge, the evidence types also shift: human EEG/MEG and traveling-wave analyses provide temporal dynamics, while REBUS remains a framework interpretation rather than direct proof of all intermediate steps.

That bridge is especially plausible because thalamus is not only a routing node but also a coordination node for cortical timing. If psychedelic states perturb thalamocortical interactions in nuclei involved in attentional coordination (for example, pulvinar-related pathways), then altered oscillatory regimes are not a separate phenomenon; they are a downstream expression of the same reweighting process [Saalmann2012; SaalmannKastner2011; Muthukumaraswamy2013; Alamia2020].

That is where oscillations, alpha suppression, and traveling-wave dynamics become essential.
