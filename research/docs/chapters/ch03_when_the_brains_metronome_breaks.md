# Chapter 3 Draft: When the Brain's Metronome Breaks

## Why Timing Is Not a Side Detail

If Chapter 2 describes a loosening of access control (gating), Chapter 3 describes what happens to the timing system that normally keeps perception coherent.

The brain does not process information continuously in a simple, uniform stream. It coordinates activity across regions using oscillatory dynamics and time-sensitive coupling. Under psychedelics, those timing relationships shift in ways that map onto altered perception, altered salience, and altered sense of reality [Kometer2013; Muthukumaraswamy2013].

This is why "the visuals" and "the insights" should not be treated as separate topics. They emerge in a brain whose temporal coordination has been reweighted.

## 1. Alpha Power: A Constraint Signal Weakens

One of the most replicated findings across serotonergic psychedelic studies is reduced alpha-band power in cortical recordings, though the exact pattern varies by compound, dose, task, and recording method [Kometer2013; Muthukumaraswamy2013].

Why does alpha matter?

In many contexts, alpha rhythms are associated with:

- inhibitory control
- selective attention
- sensory gating
- stabilization of ongoing perceptual interpretation

That does not make alpha a single "consciousness rhythm." But when alpha organization weakens, the system may become less able to suppress competing inputs and less able to maintain a stable perceptual frame.

This gives a mechanistic bridge between Chapter 2 and subjective report:

- if gating is loosening
- and rhythmic inhibition/coordination is weakening
- then more signals can compete, and fewer are cleanly filtered

## 2. Beyond Alpha: Broadband Desynchronization and State Change

Early psychedelic electrophysiology discussions often focused on one frequency band at a time. That is useful, but incomplete.

Human MEG work under psilocybin points to broader cortical desynchronization across frequency ranges, not just a narrow alpha effect [Muthukumaraswamy2013]. In plain terms, the brain's usual coordinated patterns become less orderly.

This matters because desynchronization is not merely "noise." It may reflect a shift in how cortical populations coordinate information, especially if the system is moving away from rigid, repetitive dynamics toward more flexible but less predictable ones [Muthukumaraswamy2013; CarhartHarrisFriston2019]. The second citation is a framework-level interpretation.

This is one reason psychedelic states can feel paradoxical:

- less stable, yet sometimes more vivid
- less predictable, yet sometimes more meaningful
- less organized, yet sometimes more associative

The system is not simply degraded. It is reconfigured.

## 3. Traveling Waves: Directionality Starts to Matter

Oscillations describe rhythms. Traveling-wave analyses ask a sharper question: in which direction is patterned activity moving across cortex?

That question matters because ordinary perception is not just bottom-up sensing. It also depends on top-down predictions flowing from higher-order areas toward sensory cortex.

Under DMT, EEG work has reported a shift in cortical traveling-wave dynamics, with reduced backward-wave dominance and increased forward-wave activity during the acute state [Alamia2020]. This is a strong empirical result for that paradigm and a useful bridge to predictive-processing models.

Careful wording matters here:

- strong claim: the DMT study observed a traveling-wave direction shift in that dataset [Alamia2020]
- weaker/generalized claim: the same pattern defines all psychedelic states
- framework-level claim: this directly proves predictive coding accounts in full

The first claim is solid. The second and third require caution.

## 4. Predictive Models and REBUS: Useful, Powerful, Not Final Proof

REBUS ("Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics") is one of the most influential frameworks for connecting receptor pharmacology, network dynamics, and subjective effects. It proposes that psychedelics relax the precision-weighting of high-level priors, increasing the influence of bottom-up signals and latent material [CarhartHarrisFriston2019].

This framework is powerful because it explains several features at once:

- perceptual ambiguity
- unusual salience
- rapid associative cognition
- emotional breakthrough
- destabilization of rigid beliefs

But REBUS should be used as a computational interpretation framework, not as a substitute for direct measurement of each intermediate step. The model organizes evidence well; it does not eliminate the need for compound-specific and paradigm-specific experiments.

## 5. Time Distortion Revisited: The Timing System and the Feeling of Duration

Subjective time distortion is often described phenomenologically ("time slowed," "time dissolved"), but Chapter 3 suggests a stronger framing: it is also a timing-coordination problem.

If alpha organization weakens, desynchronization increases, and top-down predictive timing becomes less dominant, then the continuity of experience may fragment into more salient micro-events. That can make minutes feel longer, sequences feel nonlinear, and ordinary temporal expectations feel unreliable [Kometer2013; Muthukumaraswamy2013; CarhartHarrisFriston2019]. The last citation is a framework interpretation.

This does not mean electrophysiology fully explains time distortion. It means timing dynamics deserve central status in the explanation rather than a footnote.

## 6. What the Evidence Is Strongest On (So Far)

### Stronger (Directly Observed in Specific Paradigms)

- reduced alpha power under psilocybin in human studies [Kometer2013]
- broadband cortical desynchronization patterns in psilocybin MEG [Muthukumaraswamy2013]
- DMT-related traveling-wave direction changes in EEG [Alamia2020]

### More Interpretive / Cross-Study Synthesis

- "top-down metronome collapse" as a unifying narrative across compounds
- direct mapping from traveling-wave changes to all subjective phenomena
- predictive-processing explanations as settled mechanism rather than framework

This distinction should stay visible throughout the project. The writing gets stronger, not weaker, when the confidence boundaries are explicit.

## 7. How Chapter 3 Connects to Therapeutic Outcomes

Why spend so much time on oscillations and timing if the project also cares about healing and lasting change?

Because a brain that has temporarily lost some of its normal synchronizing constraints may:

- admit previously suppressed material
- reassign salience to memories and beliefs
- allow competing interpretations to coexist long enough for updating

That does not guarantee improvement. But it describes a plausible transition state in which reconsolidation and plasticity mechanisms (Chapter 4) could become clinically relevant.

The short version:

- Chapter 2 explains why more gets in
- Chapter 3 explains why it no longer arrives under the same temporal rules
- Chapter 4 will ask what gets rewritten afterward

## Bridge to Chapter 4

If psychedelic states loosen constraints and scramble the brain's usual timing hierarchy, the next question is what persists after acute effects end.

That moves the project from altered consciousness to metaplasticity, reconsolidation, and the evidence for critical-period-like reopening.
