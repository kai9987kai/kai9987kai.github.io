# Source Map (Primary Sources + Key Reviews)

This file maps major claims in the drafts to papers and marks how confidently each claim is supported.

Legend:

- `High`: directly supported by the cited paper(s)
- `Medium`: supported but interpretive/generalized beyond the exact experiment
- `Speculative`: hypothesis/framework or extrapolation

## A. Receptor Binding, Kinetics, and LSD Duration

### Claim

LSD shows unusually slow dissociation kinetics, and an EL2 "lid" mechanism is a major explanation for this.

- Confidence: `High` for slow kinetics + EL2-lid mechanism in the studied receptor system
- Confidence: `Medium` for extending this to the full duration of the human LSD experience

### Source

1. Wacker D, Wang S, McCorvy JD, et al. *Crystal Structure of an LSD-Bound Human Serotonin Receptor*. Cell. 2017;168(3):377-389.e12. doi:10.1016/j.cell.2016.12.033.
   - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28129538/
   - PMCID: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5289311/

### Notes / Correction

- The 2017 structure is `5-HT2B`, not `5-HT2A`.
- The paper also reports exceptionally slow LSD dissociation at both `5-HT2B` and `5-HT2A`, and proposes the EL2 "lid" (via MD + mutational evidence) as a kinetic mechanism.
- Our drafts should keep saying this is a major contributor, not a complete explanation of trip duration.

## B. 5-HT2A Centrality in Human LSD Effects

### Claim

5-HT2A receptor signaling is central to major subjective and connectivity effects of LSD in humans.

- Confidence: `High`

### Source

1. Preller KH, Burt JB, Ji JL, et al. *Changes in global and thalamic brain connectivity in LSD-induced altered states of consciousness are attributable to the 5-HT2A receptor*. eLife. 2018;7:e35082. doi:10.7554/eLife.35082.
   - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30355445/
   - eLife: https://elifesciences.org/articles/35082

### Notes

- Strong support for the statement that LSD-related connectivity changes (including thalamic connectivity changes) and subjective effects are blocked by ketanserin in this paradigm.
- This supports a `5-HT2A`-centric mechanistic story, but does not mean every phenomenon is exclusively `5-HT2A`-mediated.

## C. Alpha Suppression, Visual Processing Changes, and 5-HT2A

### Claim

Psilocybin reduces alpha oscillatory power and alters visual processing in ways linked to 5-HT2A receptor activation.

- Confidence: `High`

### Source

1. Kometer M, Schmidt A, Jäncke L, Vollenweider FX. *Activation of serotonin 2A receptors underlies the psilocybin-induced effects on α oscillations, N170 visual-evoked potentials, and visual hallucinations*. J Neurosci. 2013;33(25):10544-10551. doi:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.3007-12.2013.
   - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23785166/
   - PMCID: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6618596/

### Notes

- Useful for claims about alpha suppression and ketanserin blockade in human psilocybin studies.
- Stronger for visual processing/phenomenology than for whole-brain network claims.

## D. Cortical Desynchronization and Deep Pyramidal Excitability Models

### Claim

The human psychedelic state (psilocybin MEG) includes broadband cortical desynchronization and may be explained by increased excitability of deep-layer pyramidal cells.

- Confidence: `High` for observed desynchronization patterns
- Confidence: `Medium` for model-based causal interpretation (DCM)

### Source

1. Muthukumaraswamy SD, Carhart-Harris RL, Moran RJ, et al. *Broadband cortical desynchronization underlies the human psychedelic state*. J Neurosci. 2013;33(38):15171-15183. doi:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.2063-13.2013.
   - PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24048847/
   - PMCID: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6618409/

### Notes

- Supports the chapter framing that timing/rhythms are not a side issue but part of the mechanism.
- DCM-based deep-pyramidal interpretation should be described as a model-supported explanation.

## E. Traveling Waves and Bottom-Up / Top-Down Reweighting

### Claim

Under DMT, cortical traveling-wave dynamics shift away from typical backward-wave predominance toward increased forward-wave dominance, consistent with reduced top-down constraint.

- Confidence: `High` for DMT traveling-wave shift in the study
- Confidence: `Medium` for generalizing to all psychedelics
- Confidence: `Speculative/Framework` for direct mapping to predictive coding in all contexts

### Source

1. Alamia A, Timmermann C, Nutt DJ, VanRullen R, Carhart-Harris RL. *DMT alters cortical travelling waves*. eLife. 2020;9:e59784. doi:10.7554/eLife.59784.
   - eLife: https://elifesciences.org/articles/59784

### Notes

- Excellent support for the "traveling waves" section, but be explicit that the paper is DMT-specific and EEG-based.
- Use careful wording when extending this to psilocybin/LSD without direct replication.

## H. Critical-Period Reopening / Metaplasticity Window (Preclinical, Cross-Compound)

### Claim

Multiple psychedelics (and ketamine/ibogaine in this framework) can reopen a social reward learning critical period in adult mice, with different durations and ECM-related transcriptional signatures.

- Confidence: `High` for the mouse social reward learning model and measured time-course differences
- Confidence: `Medium` for extrapolating timing windows directly to humans

### Source

1. Nardou R, Sawyer E, Song YJ, et al. *Psychedelics reopen the social reward learning critical period*. Nature. 2023;618:790-798. doi:10.1038/s41586-023-06204-3.
   - Nature: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06204-3

### Notes

- Supports the "integration period" and ECM/metaplasticity discussion when framed as preclinical evidence.
- Keep human claims cautious and separate from mouse timeline claims.
