The kai9987kai Blog Lab: building strange web ideas into real projects.

This is the headline post for the blog: an introduction to the creative technology direction behind kai9987kai, from AI tools and Three.js scenes to AR prototypes, browser games, maker workflows and portfolio-ready experiments.

The kai9987kai Blog Lab is built around one simple idea: strange ideas are worth building. A small web experiment, a rough game prototype, a 3D scene, a prompt tool or a maker utility can all become something more useful when it is tested, improved and explained properly.

This blog is the place to collect those experiments and turn them into a clearer story. Instead of leaving projects as random files or forgotten prototypes, each post can show the idea, the build, the upgrade and the next step.

The goal is not just to make pages. The goal is to make ideas visible, usable and worth improving.

The mission

kai9987kai is best framed as a creative technology identity. That means building with code, but also thinking about design, usability, visual polish, interaction and presentation. The strongest projects are not just technically working. They are understandable, memorable and easy to explore.

The Blog Lab should show that process. A visitor should be able to see what each project is, why it exists, what was improved, and what could be added next. That makes the site feel like a living portfolio instead of a static template.

Headline idea:

Every post should work like a mini case study: what the project is, what changed, what works well, and what the next version could become.

Main project lanes

The blog works best when the projects are grouped into clear lanes. This makes the homepage easier to understand and helps each post feel connected to the bigger kai9987kai identity.

AI tools Prompt helpers, idea generators, assistant-style interfaces and creative dashboards.
3D web labs Three.js scenes, model viewers, interactive objects, cameras, lighting and WebGL polish.
AR prototypes Marker scanning, WebAR experiments, mobile testing flows and visual troubleshooting.
Maker workflows 3D printing tools, G-code ideas, hardware concepts and practical browser utilities.

The blog format

A strong post needs more than a title and an image. It should explain the project clearly enough that someone can understand the thinking behind it. That is especially useful for portfolio work, because it shows the design process as well as the final result.

The format can stay consistent across the whole blog: introduce the idea, explain the problem, show the build direction, list the upgrades, then finish with next steps. That structure works for games, AI tools, AR pages, Three.js labs and maker projects.

  • Idea: what inspired the project and what it is meant to do.
  • Build: what tools, code or design choices were used.
  • Upgrade: what was improved compared with the basic version.
  • Next: what could make the project more useful, polished or impressive.

What comes next

The next step is to keep replacing template posts with real kai9987kai articles. The blog should grow into a project archive where each page shows a different skill: UI design, JavaScript, Three.js, WebAR, AI interface design, game mechanics, 3D printing workflows and creative problem solving.

Over time, the homepage can become a timeline of experiments. It can show what has been built, what is being improved, and what ideas are still waiting to become working demos.

Portfolio angle:

This headline post gives the whole blog a purpose. It explains the creative direction, connects the post series together and makes the site feel more like a serious project hub.
